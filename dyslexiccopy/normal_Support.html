Tips to Support Dyslexic Minds

If your child has dyslexia, they will think differently, creatively, and laterally. And they possess the precise skills required for the future. Here’s how you can support them to be their best at home.

5 Tips to Support Dyslexic Minds

Reading

  • Listen to audiobooks while reading aloud to your child.
  • Make sure they read loudly and quietly to themselves for a while.
  • Reread their preferred book. It aids in their learning.
  • Take turns reading aloud from books as a family.
  • Discuss the stories you've read aloud and ask questions like.
  • Use textbooks, or also explore comic books and graphic novels...

Visual Strength

  • Aids to help in improving their studying abilities, as well as improving their attention spans by Watching, listening, touching, and learning through movement.
  • Use a tape recorder: provide access to audiobooks.
  • Present a small amount of work.
  • Highlight essential information.
  • Develop reading guides. A reading guide provides the student with a road map of what is written and features periodic questions to help him or her focus on relevant content.

Make learning playful

  • Makeup songs, poems, and even dances to help remember things.
  • Play word games.
  • If your child is younger, use nursery rhymes and play silly rhyming games.

Schoolwork

  • Closely collaborate with your child's school: Make an effort to secure the services the kid requires. Establish an IEP (individualized Educational Plan) with the school that outlines the child's needs and enables you to check up on their progress.
  • Utilize technology using desktops, tablets, and smartphones. Online dictionaries, spellcheckers, and text-to-speech programs can all have a big impact on how well they do.
  • Organize your schoolwork: Dyslexia makes it challenging to maintain organization. Help the child break down difficult chores into manageable parts.

Emotional Support

Be firm, patient, and positive, and give the child time to do things besides schoolwork.

  • Celebrate successes: Spend a day together having fun towards the end of a project or after a difficult test.
  • Do not look for perfection: Close enough is frequently a great success.
  • Assist your kid in understanding what dyslexia is: They should understand that it is not their fault and that you will work through it together.
  • Let your child participate in activities that they enjoy and are good at this can help to balance the difficulties of academics.
  • Acknowledge their child’s abilities and skills. Do not let learning difficulties dominate their thoughts.
  • Remind your youngster that many incredibly great people, from Albert Einstein to Whoopi Goldberg, have dyslexia.
  • Tell them “I love you” often.
